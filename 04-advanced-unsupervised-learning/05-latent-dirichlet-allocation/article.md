# Latent Dirichlet Allocation — Topics That Emerge From Text

### *Algorithms in Python --- Advanced Unsupervised Learning, Part 5*

---

In Part 6 of the basic Unsupervised track we built
Non-negative Matrix Factorisation — the additive,
parts-based decomposition behind classical topic modelling.
NMF gave us topics-as-word-distributions and
documents-as-topic-combinations, but the framework was
algebraic: minimise a reconstruction loss subject to
non-negativity.

**Latent Dirichlet Allocation** (Blei, Ng & Jordan, 2003) is
the *probabilistic* cousin of NMF for topic modelling. It
assumes a generative process that explains how each document
was written — *first* sample a topic mixture for the
document, *then* for each word independently sample a topic
from that mixture, *then* sample a word from that topic's
word distribution. The model is fit by Bayesian inference
(typically variational EM in production, Gibbs sampling for
research) to find the topic distributions and per-document
mixtures most consistent with the observed corpus.

LDA dominated topic modelling for the decade after its
publication. It was the standard model behind Google's news
clustering, Twitter trend analysis, scientific-paper topic
maps, and academic recommender systems. Modern neural
embeddings (BERTopic, top2vec, dense-retrieval clustering)
have largely displaced it for cutting-edge work — but
LDA remains the canonical reference point, the interpretable
baseline every modern topic model is compared against, and
the algorithm most likely to be still running quietly in a
production stack you didn't know about.

---

## The generative model

LDA assumes a corpus was generated by the following process:

```
For each topic k = 1..K:
    Sample word distribution φ_k ~ Dirichlet(β)

For each document d = 1..D:
    Sample topic mixture θ_d ~ Dirichlet(α)
    For each word position i = 1..N_d:
        Sample topic z_{d,i} ~ Categorical(θ_d)
        Sample word w_{d,i} ~ Categorical(φ_{z_{d,i}})
```

Read top-down. Each **topic** `k` is a distribution over the
vocabulary — `φ_k` is a vector of length `V` (vocabulary
size) that sums to 1. "Sports" is a topic whose `φ` puts
high probability on words like *goal*, *match*, *team*;
"Cooking" is a topic whose `φ` peaks on *recipe*, *oven*,
*flour*.

Each **document** `d` is a mixture of topics. `θ_d` is a
vector of length `K` that sums to 1 — "this document is
60% sports, 30% business, 10% politics". The Dirichlet prior
controls how concentrated this mixture is: small `α` favours
mixtures focused on one or two topics, large `α` favours
broad mixtures.

Each **word** is generated by first sampling a topic from
the document's topic mixture, then sampling a word from that
topic's word distribution.

The observed data is just the bag-of-words representation of
each document. Inference recovers `φ`, `θ`, and (implicitly)
the topic assignments `z` that most plausibly generated the
observed words.

---

## Why the Dirichlet?

The **Dirichlet distribution** is the conjugate prior for the
Categorical / Multinomial. In plain English: if you have a
Dirichlet prior over a distribution and observe data from a
Categorical, the posterior is still a Dirichlet — and the
update is just adding the observed counts to the prior
parameters. This conjugacy makes Bayesian updates
tractable.

The Dirichlet is parameterised by a vector `α`. Small
components encourage sparse distributions (most mass on a
few categories); large components encourage uniform
distributions (mass spread evenly). In LDA:

- `α` controls per-document topic concentration. Default
  `α = 1/K` favours documents that are mixtures of a few
  topics, not many.
- `β` controls per-topic word concentration. Default
  `β = 1/K` favours topics that put mass on a few words,
  not all words equally.

These priors are *hyperparameters* — set in advance, or
optimised via empirical Bayes. They are the main knobs in
practice; the more data you have, the less they matter.

---

## Inference: variational EM

The exact posterior over `φ`, `θ`, `z` given the observed
words is analytically intractable — the joint involves a
sum over all possible topic assignments. Two standard
approaches:

**Collapsed Gibbs sampling** (Griffiths & Steyvers, 2004).
Marginalise out `φ` and `θ`, leaving only `z`. Sample each
topic assignment `z_{d,i}` conditioned on all the others and
the observed words. Eventually the chain converges to the
posterior. Simple to implement, slow to converge, the
research default.

**Variational EM** (the original Blei et al paper).
Approximate the posterior with a simpler distribution `q`
that factorises across documents, then minimise the
KL-divergence between `q` and the true posterior. The
algorithm alternates two steps:

- **E-step**: optimise the per-document variational
  parameters (the approximate posterior over each `θ_d` and
  each `z_{d,i}`).
- **M-step**: re-estimate the global topic-word
  distributions `φ_k`.

Iterate until the *evidence lower bound* (ELBO) — the
quantity variational inference maximises — stops improving.
This is the algorithm sklearn uses by default and the right
choice for production-scale topic modelling.

The variational approximation can be wrong (it's
approximate, after all), but it's typically wrong in
predictable, well-understood ways — most importantly, it
under-estimates posterior variance.

## A worked example

The companion script trains LDA on a 12-document corpus
constructed from three deliberately-distinct topics: cooking,
machine learning, and sports. The vocabulary is small (~30
unique words) so the topic recovery is clearly verifiable.

```
DEMO 1 --- LDA on a 12-document toy corpus
  Vocabulary size : 33
  Documents       : 12
  Topics          : 3
  Method          : variational EM (sklearn)
  Converged in    : 100 iterations
  Perplexity      : 27.33
```

```
DEMO 2 --- Top words per topic
  Topic 0: weight, dataset, train, network, loss, learning, model, neural, layer, accuracy
  Topic 1: recipe, flour, oven, butter, bake, egg, dough, mix, sugar, cook
  Topic 2: win, team, score, player, match, coach, game, goal, league, ball
```

```
DEMO 3 --- Per-document topic mixture (rounded; columns reordered here to match the label names)
  doc  cooking  ml      sports  inferred-label
  ---  -------  ------  ------  --------------
   0   0.95     0.02    0.02    cooking
   1   0.94     0.03    0.03    cooking
   2   0.95     0.03    0.03    cooking
   3   0.94     0.03    0.03    cooking
   4   0.03     0.94    0.03    ml
   5   0.03     0.94    0.03    ml
   6   0.03     0.94    0.03    ml
   7   0.03     0.94    0.03    ml
   8   0.03     0.03    0.94    sports
   9   0.03     0.03    0.94    sports
  10   0.03     0.03    0.94    sports
  11   0.03     0.03    0.94    sports
```

Three observations.

**LDA recovers all three topics perfectly.** The top words
per topic exactly match the three planted themes —
*recipe / flour / oven* for cooking, *model / train / neural*
for machine learning, *goal / match / team* for sports. On
real data the topics are rarely this clean, but the recipe
is the same.

**Per-document mixtures are sharp because the corpus is
clean.** Each document loads 90%+ on its true topic. Real
documents would have more mixed weights — a sports article
discussing the *business* of a football club would have
non-trivial weight on both sports and business topics.

**Perplexity is the standard quality metric.** It is
`exp(-log-likelihood / total-word-count)`. Lower is better.
It is comparable across models with the same vocabulary,
but not across vocabularies. For real-world LDA, perplexity
scores between 100 and 500 are typical depending on corpus
quality and topic count.

## Big-O and complexity

![[BIG-O TABLE IMAGE]]

LDA's cost is dominated by inference:

**Variational EM training**: roughly `O(I · D · N̄ · K)` in
total, where `I` is the number of EM iterations, `D` is
the document count, `N̄` is the average document length, and
`K` is the number of topics. Modest cost — scales to
millions of documents with online learning variants (Hoffman
et al, 2010).

**Inference for new documents**: `O(N · K)` — assign topic
probabilities to each word of a new document under the
fitted model.

**Memory**: `O(D · K + K · V)` for the topic mixtures and
the topic-word distributions.

For genuinely large corpora (>10⁶ documents), **online LDA**
or stochastic variational inference are the production
defaults. They process documents in mini-batches like SGD,
giving near-linear scalability.

## Real-world ML and AI connections

**News and document clustering.** Google News' topic
clustering pipelines used LDA-style methods through much of
the 2010s. Modern systems mostly switched to dense
embeddings (BERT, sentence transformers), but LDA is the
historical baseline.

**Scientific literature mining.** LDA-based topic maps of
arXiv, PubMed, the Web of Science — used to spot emerging
research trends, identify under-served research areas, and
recommend related papers.

**Customer support and review analysis.** Run LDA on a
month of support tickets; the topics tell you what your
users are struggling with this week. Still widely used as
the cheap, interpretable baseline in customer-experience
analytics.

**Social media topic detection.** Twitter, Reddit, Hacker
News — LDA was the workhorse trend-detector for a decade.

**Recommender systems with content-based features.** Map
each item to its topic mixture under a fitted LDA; recommend
items with similar topic profiles to what the user has
historically liked. Cheap, transparent, and surprisingly
competitive against neural CF on cold-start problems.

**Foundations of probabilistic programming.** LDA is the
canonical "complex probabilistic model" used in tutorials
for PyMC, Pyro, Stan, and other PPL systems. Implementing
LDA cleanly is a rite of passage in the
probabilistic-modelling community.

The pattern: LDA is the *interpretable baseline* for
topic modelling. Modern neural methods often beat it on
some metrics — but rarely on interpretability, and almost
never on the question "what is this topic actually about?".

## When NOT to use LDA

**When you have lots of data and computer-vision-style
infrastructure.** Train a sentence transformer (or
fine-tune one), cluster embeddings with HDBSCAN. The
modern BERTopic / top2vec pipeline. Beats LDA on most
benchmarks, costs more compute.

**When documents are short (tweets, queries, log lines).**
LDA needs ≥50 words per document to converge cleanly.
For short text, switch to biterm topic models, sentence
embeddings + clustering, or dedicated short-text methods.

**When you need probabilistic predictions on new
documents.** LDA's variational posterior on new docs
under-estimates uncertainty. Use full Bayesian inference
(MCMC) if calibrated probabilities matter.

**When topic *quality* matters more than topic count.**
LDA's "topic 7" might not be coherent — it could be
spurious correlations or noise. Use topic-coherence
metrics (UMass, UCI) to filter; better still, use a
modern dense-embedding clustering approach which tends
to produce more semantically-meaningful clusters.

machine learning, sports — and verifies the inferred
topics, per-document mixtures, and perplexity. The headline
insight worth pinning to the wall: **LDA is a generative
probabilistic model in which documents are mixtures of
topics and topics are distributions over words; variational
EM infers the most plausible topics and mixtures from
observed bag-of-words data; the result is interpretable,
calibrated, and the historical baseline for everything
modern topic modelling has tried to beat**.
